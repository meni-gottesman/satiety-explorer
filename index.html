How filling is your food?

Some foods keep you full on very few calories; others pack a lot of calories into every bite. This chart maps 292 whole, unprocessed foods by how much fullness you get per calorie. Each dot is a food - the higher it sits, the more filling it is for its calories.

About & how to read this

New here? How to read this chart

Every dot is one food. Two things decide where it sits:

  • Up / down – fullness per calorie. Higher means more filling for the same calories. Foods near the top (leafy greens, berries, white fish) fill you up cheaply; foods near the bottom are calorie-dense for how full they leave you.
  • Left / right – energy density, the calories packed into each gram. Foods on the left are watery and bulky; foods on the right are concentrated.

The best foods for staying full on fewer calories sit in the top-left. Colours mark five tiers from very high (green) to very low (red) fullness per calorie – or switch Colour to group by food category. Turn on Trend lines to see the average pattern for each category.

Try it: search a food, pick a category, drag a box to zoom, or double-click to reset.

Where the data and the science come from

The foods and their nutrition facts come from open, established databases; the fullness score comes from published satiety research.

  • Food data – Open Food Facts, a free crowd-sourced database of millions of products worldwide. We keep the truly unprocessed whole foods (NOVA group 1) in every language, translating non-English names so the same food merges across languages (French tomates, German tomaten and English tomatoes become one point).
  • Nutrient reference – USDA FoodData Central.
  • How filling a food is – the Satiety Index of Holt et al. (1995), the landmark study that measured how full real foods make people feel, and the Fullness Factor regression it inspired, which predicts satiety from calories, protein, fibre and fat.
  • Why energy density matters – research by Barbara Rolls and colleagues showing people tend to eat a fairly fixed weight of food, so less energy-dense foods mean fewer calories for the same fullness.
  • Processing level – the NOVA classification (Monteiro et al.), used to keep only whole, unprocessed foods.

The full method and validation are in the technical paper (PDF).

Why this is about as accurate as a model like this can be

The score is built from the factors satiety research has shown matter most – and nothing it can't justify:

  • It uses the strongest predictors. Across studies, energy density and protein and fibre are the most consistent drivers of how filling a food is – exactly the inputs this model uses.
  • It was validated, not just asserted. The formula was fitted and cross-validated against published satiety-index data. Adding more variables did not improve out-of-sample accuracy, so it sits close to the practical ceiling for any score computed from a nutrition label alone.
  • The ranking is robust. Scores are percentile ranks, so a few odd data points can't distort the scale and every food is directly comparable. Each food counts once, however many label variants it merged.
  • The data is cleaned. We keep only whole, unprocessed foods and reconcile every food's calories against its protein, carbs and fat, dropping entries that don't add up.

In short, for a score you can compute from any nutrition label, this is about as accurate as it gets – the limitations below cover what no label-based score can do.

Limitations – what this can and can't tell you
  • Fullness is personal. It depends on you, the meal, how a food is prepared, and habit. These scores are a population-level estimate, not a promise about any one person or meal.
  • It's a relative ranking. A score of 80 means "more filling per calorie than about 80% of these foods", not an absolute amount of fullness.
  • It only sees the label. Working from calories, protein, fibre and fat, it can't fully capture texture, how solid or liquid a food is, blood-sugar response, or how foods combine in a meal.
  • Crowd-sourced data has errors. Open Food Facts is community-entered; we filter and sanity-check, but some mistakes will slip through.
  • It's a slice, not everything. This view is unprocessed whole foods (names translated to English) – not every food in the world.
  • Trend curves are a summary. Each category's curve is one smooth average shape, so individual foods can sit well above or below it.

This is for general interest and ranking – not medical or dietary advice.
